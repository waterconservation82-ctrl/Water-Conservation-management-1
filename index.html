Introduction

Slide 2

Why water matters and the global availability snapshot.

  • Water is essential for life, agriculture, industry and ecosystems.
  • Only about 2.5% of Earth’s water is freshwater; less than 1% is readily usable.
  • Water scarcity and demand are rising due to population growth and climate change.

What is Water Conservation?

Slide 3

Short definition and objectives.

  • Efficient use of water by reducing waste and losses.
  • Protects and manages water resources for present and future generations.

Importance

Slide 4
  • Prevents water shortages and supports agriculture.
  • Maintains groundwater levels and aquatic ecosystems.
  • Reduces the social and economic impacts of droughts.
Key message:

Saving water safeguards food security, health and biodiversity.

Causes of Water Scarcity

Slide 5
  • Over-extraction of groundwater and unsustainable withdrawals.
  • Population growth, urbanization, and industrialization.
  • Pollution of freshwater sources and loss of catchment forests.
  • Climate change leading to changing rainfall patterns and droughts.

Methods of Conservation

Slide 6

Actionable steps across households, agriculture and community.

Household

  • Fix leaks, use water-efficient fixtures, reuse greywater where safe.
  • Shorter showers, bucket for washing, efficient appliances.

Agriculture

  • Drip and sprinkler irrigation, mulching, and scheduling irrigation by crop needs.

Community & Policy

  • Wastewater reuse, pricing reforms, incentive programs for farmers.

Rainwater Harvesting

Slide 7

Capture and store rainwater for use and groundwater recharge.

  • Rooftop harvesting, surface run-off capture and storage in tanks/ponds.
  • Low-cost, scalable — suitable for homes, schools and public buildings.
  • Recharges groundwater and reduces dependency on seasonal rainfall.

Traditional Water Conservation Techniques

Slide 13

Proven local systems from across India — useful alongside modern methods.

  • Johads (Rajasthan) — earthen check dams to harvest monsoon runoff.
  • Baolis / Stepwells — deep structures storing water and recharging aquifers.
  • Eri system (Tamil Nadu) — tank irrigation that supports local farming.
  • Zing (Ladakh) — glacier-melt channels that store and distribute water.
  • Ahar-Pyne (Bihar) — floodwater collection and distribution systems.

Watershed Management

Slide 8

Land and water planning across a catchment to maximize retention and reduce erosion.

  • Construction of check dams, contour bunding and micro-catchments.
  • Afforestation, soil conservation and sustainable land use planning.
  • Benefits: improved groundwater recharge, reduced floods and better agriculture.

Modern Technologies in Water Management

Slide 9
  • Smart irrigation controllers, soil moisture sensors and precision irrigation.
  • GIS & remote sensing for watershed and resource mapping.
  • Wastewater treatment and recycling, desalination where feasible.

Government Initiatives

Slide 11

Major national schemes and programs (examples from India).

  • Jal Shakti Abhiyan — groundwater recharge and rainwater harvesting drives.
  • Namami Gange — river cleaning & river-front rejuvenation.
  • Atal Bhujal Yojana — community-based groundwater management.
  • MGNREGA — funds used for water conservation structures (pans, check dams).
  • Swachh Bharat — sanitation improvements that protect water sources.

Community Participation

Slide 12
  • Local groups can clean and maintain ponds, stepwells and canals.
  • Community rainwater harvesting and shared recharge structures.
  • Awareness campaigns, school programs and youth-led initiatives.

Conclusion & Suggested Actions

Slide 10 / 13
  • Use water wisely: fix leaks, adopt rainwater harvesting, and support efficient irrigation.
  • Combine traditional and modern techniques for resilient water systems.
  • Collective action by government, communities and individuals is required for a sustainable water future.
